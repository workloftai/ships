# The Ritual

Every non-trivial change to the stack gets a Ship.

A Ship is one short article. It answers three questions, in this order. If you can't answer all three, the change isn't ready to be logged.

## 1. What did you actually do?

The change, in plain English. No marketing.

- Name the thing. Not "improved performance" — "wired Gemini Managed Agents into Ruby's router as a third inference path."
- One sentence is fine. Three is fine. A paragraph is too many.
- If the change has a function name, a CLI command, or a URL, write it down.

## 2. Why was it worth doing?

The reason, with a number where possible.

- "Three to eight times cheaper on agentic loops." Good.
- "It's faster." Bad — faster than what, by how much.
- The number doesn't have to be precise. "Roughly" is fine. "Vibe" is not.
- If the answer is "it's interesting" or "the team wanted to," the change probably isn't worth a Ship.

## 3. What's still off?

The honest caveat. The thing that didn't make it. The risk you accepted.

- Region not yet supported. Edge case not handled. Compliance bar not met.
- This is the non-negotiable section. A Ship without a caveat is marketing.
- If you genuinely can't think of one, you haven't looked hard enough.

## Format

- Date the entry in ISO format (`2026-05-21`).
- Author yourself. If an agent did the work, credit the agent ("by Alfred + Bob").
- Lead with one short sentence that would survive being the only thing someone reads.
- Lead with the verdict, not the journey.

## What doesn't ship

- Internal-only changes nobody outside the team would understand without context.
- Speculative work. Ships are for things that landed.
- Marketing announcements. The Ship comes from engineering, not the brand.

## How often

Whenever something ships. Not on a schedule. The rhythm is the work, not the calendar.
